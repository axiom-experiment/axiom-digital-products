### S1-P01 — The Deep-Dive Blog Post Architect
**When to use:** When you need a complete, expert-level blog post on any topic in your niche.

**The Prompt:**
```
You are an expert [YOUR NICHE] writer with 10 years of hands-on experience. Write a deep-dive blog post titled '[YOUR TITLE]' for an audience of [DESCRIBE YOUR READER — e.g., 'freelance designers who want to raise their rates'].

The post must:
- Open with a hook that identifies a specific pain point or counterintuitive insight (no generic intros)
- Include exactly 5 H2 sections, each with 2-3 substantive paragraphs
- Feature at least one specific, actionable tip per section (real tactics, not vague advice)
- Include a concrete example or case study (you can create a realistic fictional example)
- End with a 'Key Takeaways' section and a single clear call-to-action
- Target approximately 1,200 words
- Write in a confident, direct, conversational tone — like a trusted expert talking to a peer

Do not use filler phrases like 'In today's fast-paced world' or 'It's no secret that'. Start directly with the hook.
```

**Customization Notes:** Replace [YOUR NICHE], [YOUR TITLE], and [DESCRIBE YOUR READER]. Adjust the style note to match your brand voice.

**What you'll get:** A fully structured ~1,200-word blog post with a punchy opening, five meaty sections, real tactics, and a strong CTA.

### S1-P02 — The Content Repurposer (1 Article → 7 Formats)
**When to use:** When you've written a blog post and need a week's worth of social content from it.

**The Prompt:**
```
I've written the following article. Repurpose it into 7 different content formats for different channels. Here's the article:

[PASTE YOUR ARTICLE]

Create the following:
1. Twitter/X thread (8-10 tweets, starts with a bold hook, ends with a CTA to the article)
2. LinkedIn post (250-350 words, professional tone, first-person, ends with a question to drive comments)
3. Instagram caption (150 words max, emotional hook, 5 relevant hashtags)
4. Email newsletter section (200 words, first-person, conversational, links to full article)
5. 3 standalone 'tips' formatted as short social posts (under 100 words each)
6. YouTube/podcast talking points (5 bullet points, 30-60 seconds each)
7. Pinterest description (100 words, SEO-optimized, searchable language)

Maintain the core ideas but adapt tone and format for each platform.
```

**Customization Notes:** Optionally specify your brand's tone per platform.

**What you'll get:** Seven distinct content pieces ready to schedule — a full week of social from one piece of writing.

### S1-P03 — The Newsletter Issue Builder
**When to use:** Writing your weekly or biweekly email newsletter.

**The Prompt:**
```
Write a newsletter issue for my [NICHE] newsletter called '[NEWSLETTER NAME]'. This issue should cover the theme: [THIS WEEK'S THEME OR TOPIC].

My audience is: [DESCRIBE AUDIENCE]
My newsletter style is: [e.g., 'conversational and honest, like a letter from a smart friend']

Structure the issue as follows:
- Subject line (3 options: one curiosity, one benefit, one contrarian)
- Preview text (under 90 characters)
- Personal opening: 2-3 sentences about something real from my week that connects to the theme
- Main insight or lesson: 300-400 words — the most valuable thing my reader will learn this issue
- One 'Quick Win': a single tactical action the reader can take in under 15 minutes
- Recommendation (optional): A tool, book, or resource with a 1-sentence reason why
- Closing line that feels human, not corporate

Make it feel like it was written by a real person who genuinely cares about their readers — not a content machine.
```

**Customization Notes:** The 'personal opening' prompt works best when you give Claude a real detail from your week.

**What you'll get:** A complete newsletter issue with 3 subject line options, full content sections, and a human-feeling sign-off.

### S1-P04 — The YouTube Video Script Writer
**When to use:** Creating a 7-12 minute educational or tutorial YouTube video.

**The Prompt:**
```
Write a YouTube video script for a [7-10] minute educational video titled: '[VIDEO TITLE]'

Channel audience: [DESCRIBE]
Channel tone: [e.g., 'direct, no-fluff, occasionally funny']

Script structure:

- CTA (9:45-10:00): Specific next action (subscribe, comment with X, watch next video)

Include [VISUAL CUE] notes throughout so I know when to show something on screen.
Write it as if it will be spoken aloud — conversational, not formal.
```

**Customization Notes:** Adjust the time markers for your target length. Add your channel name to the CTA section.

**What you'll get:** A fully written, speakable video script with timing markers, visual cues, and a strong opening hook.

### S1-P05 — The Case Study Writer
**When to use:** Turning a client win into a compelling case study for your website or portfolio.

**The Prompt:**
```
Write a professional case study based on the following client project details:

Client type/industry: [DESCRIBE WITHOUT IDENTIFYING DETAILS IF NEEDED]
The challenge they faced: [DESCRIBE]
What you did: [YOUR SPECIFIC APPROACH AND ACTIONS]
The results achieved: [METRICS OR QUALITATIVE OUTCOMES]
Timeline: [HOW LONG THE PROJECT TOOK]

Write the case study in this structure:
1. Compelling headline that leads with the result, not the process
2. The Challenge (150 words): What was at stake and why it mattered
3. The Approach (200 words): What you did and why — specific, credible, avoids jargon
4. The Results (100 words): Lead with numbers where possible; if no numbers, use specific qualitative outcomes
5. Quote from client (if I provide one) or a sentence summarizing client feedback
6. What this means for similar clients (50 words): Who should reach out if they have this problem

Tone: confident, specific, human — reads like it was written by a real practitioner who is proud of this work.
```

**Customization Notes:** If the client is confidential, replace with 'A mid-sized e-commerce brand' etc. The more specific your inputs, the more powerful the output.

**What you'll get:** A 500-600 word case study ready for your website with a result-led headline and credibility-building specifics.

### S1-P06 — The Headline Generator (25 Options)
**When to use:** Whenever you need a great title for a blog post, email, video, or landing page.

**The Prompt:**
```
Generate 25 headline options for content about: [YOUR TOPIC]
Target audience: [DESCRIBE]
Content type: [blog post / email subject / YouTube title / landing page]

Generate 5 headlines in each of these 5 frameworks:
1. NUMBER + RESULT ('7 Ways to Double Your Freelance Rate in 90 Days')
2. COUNTERINTUITIVE ('Why Working Less Made Me a Better Freelancer')
3. HOW-TO + SPECIFIC OUTCOME ('How to Write a Cold Email That Gets a 40% Reply Rate')
4. CHALLENGE/FEAR + SOLUTION ('Scared of Raising Your Prices? Read This First')
5. CURIOSITY/MYSTERY ('The One Email I Send Every New Client That Changes Everything')

For each headline, score it 1-10 for: Clarity, Curiosity, and Specificity. Bold the top 3 overall.

Do not use vague language like 'amazing', 'incredible', or 'game-changing'. Every headline must be specific enough that the reader knows exactly what they'll get.
```

**Customization Notes:** Add 'Must be under 60 characters' for SEO meta titles.

**What you'll get:** 25 headline options organized by framework, each scored across 3 dimensions, with top 3 bolded.

### S2-P01 — The Launch Email Sequence (5 Emails)
**When to use:** Launching a new product, service, course, or offer to your email list.

**The Prompt:**
```
Write a 5-email launch sequence for my new offer: [DESCRIBE YOUR OFFER — what it is, who it's for, price, what they get].

My email list size: [SIZE]
Launch window: [NUMBER OF DAYS]
Biggest objection my audience has: [MAIN OBJECTION]

Email 1 (3 days before launch): Pre-launch 'tease' — build curiosity without revealing everything.
Email 2 (1 day before): 'It's almost here' — go deep on the problem this offer solves. No pitch yet.
Email 3 (Launch day, AM): The launch email — full offer reveal. Lead with transformation, not features. Clear CTA.
Email 4 (Launch day, PM or Day 2): Social proof / story email — a result, testimonial, or behind-the-scenes.
Email 5 (Final day, close): Urgency email — honest reason for deadline, clear last chance CTA. No fake scarcity.

For each email: Subject line (+ open rate variant), preview text, full body (200-400 words), CTA button text.
Tone: excited but grounded. No hype. No false urgency.
```

**Customization Notes:** If you have real testimonials, insert them into Email 4. Fake social proof is never acceptable.

**What you'll get:** A complete 5-email launch campaign with copy ready to paste into your email platform.

### S2-P02 — The Cold Outreach Email (That Doesn't Sound Cold)
**When to use:** Reaching out to potential collaborators, podcast hosts, press, or ideal clients via email.

**The Prompt:**
```
Write a cold outreach email to [TYPE OF PERSON — e.g., 'a podcast host in the personal finance space'].

My name: [NAME]
My context: [WHAT I DO in one sentence]
Why I'm reaching out: [SPECIFIC REASON]
What I bring to them: [CONCRETE VALUE — why is this good for THEM?]
Specific detail about them I noticed: [ONE SPECIFIC THING — episode, article, post — shows I've actually engaged with their work]

Write an email that:
- Is under 150 words
- Leads with something specific about them (not generic flattery)
- States what I'm proposing in one clear sentence
- Makes the value to THEM explicit
- Has a single, low-friction CTA ('Does a 20-minute call work for you this week or next?')
- Sounds like a human wrote it, not a template

Also provide a 3-sentence follow-up email for if they don't respond in 5 days.
```

**Customization Notes:** Replace the [SPECIFIC DETAIL] with something real — this is the part that gets replies. Never use a generic compliment.

**What you'll get:** A concise, human-feeling cold email and a short follow-up — two assets for your outreach campaign.

## Pro Tips for Getting the Most From This Kit

**1. Give more context, get better output.**
The more specific your inputs, the more useful the outputs. "Freelance brand photographer targeting female small business owners" beats "photographer" every time.

**2. Use it iteratively.**
Run the prompt → read the output → follow up with "make section 2 more specific" or "rewrite in a warmer tone." The first pass is a draft, not the final product.

**3. Combine prompts.**
Use S1-P01 (Deep-Dive Blog Post) → then S1-P02 (Content Repurposer) → then S1-P09 (SEO Optimizer) in sequence for a complete content workflow.

**4. Save your customized versions.**
Once you've filled in the brackets for your business, save your version of each prompt. Build your own library.

**5. Tweak for your voice.**
The outputs are raw material. Read everything aloud before publishing. If it doesn't sound like you, tell the AI: "Rewrite this in the style of the sample I'm about to share" — then paste a paragraph from your existing writing.
